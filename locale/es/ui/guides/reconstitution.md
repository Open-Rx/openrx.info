## Reconstitution

Reconstitution means dissolving a freeze-dried peptide powder in bacteriostatic water to make an injectable solution. The ratio of water to powder determines your concentration, which determines how much you draw for each dose.

Sterile technique matters here. A contaminated vial can cause serious infection. Work on a clean surface, don't rush, and don't skip the swab steps.

### What you need

Peptide vial, bacteriostatic water (BAC water), a syringe and needle for mixing, alcohol swabs, sharps container. Let refrigerated vials come to room temperature before you start.

### Steps

**1. Wash your hands.** Soap and water, 20 seconds, dry completely.

**2. Clean the vial tops.** Snap off the plastic caps. Scrub the rubber stopper on each vial with a fresh alcohol swab for about 10 seconds, then let it dry. Don't touch the stoppers after.

**3. Draw your BAC water.** Decide how much water you want to use. Pull air equal to that volume into the syringe, insert into the BAC water stopper, push the air in, flip the vial upside down, draw the water out.

**4. Add water to the peptide vial.** Insert the needle through the stopper and angle the bevel against the inside glass wall. Push the plunger slowly so the liquid runs down the side rather than blasting into the powder. This keeps it from foaming and protects the peptide.

**5. Mix.** Pull the needle out and drop the syringe in the sharps container. Gently swirl the vial or roll it between your palms until the powder dissolves and the solution is clear. Don't shake it — shaking causes foam and can break down the peptide.

**6. Check the solution.** Hold it up to a light. It should be completely clear, no cloudiness, no particles. If something looks off, discard it.

**7. Label the vial.** Write the date, the concentration (e.g., 5 mg/mL), and the expiration date. Reconstituted peptides are generally good for 28 days refrigerated.
